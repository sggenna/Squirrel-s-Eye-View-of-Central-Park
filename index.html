Flee rate vs annual human visits by park zone (Conservancy entrance data, 2009)

Key finding: Zones receiving 4M+ annual visits (Wollman Rink, SW Corner/Merchants Gate, Great Lawn) show flee rates 2.5 to 3× higher than quieter northern zones. The pattern suggests sustained human density creates two distinct squirrel populations: a habituated majority (indifferent) and a stress-conditioned minority that flees immediately. Both the 2018 Census and 2009 Conservancy survey point to the same southern concentration of activity as the driver.

About the foot traffic data: No real-time sensor exists inside Central Park. The human visit data used here comes from the 2009 Central Park Conservancy Visitor Survey, the most comprehensive study ever conducted, based on nearly 4,600 entrance counts and 3,300+ exit interviews across all seasons. It estimated 37 to 38M annual visits, with entrance-level breakdowns used to assign zone-by-zone traffic volumes. The squirrel data is from the 2018 NYC Squirrel Census, a volunteer count of 3,023 squirrels across 350 hectares. The two datasets are 9 years apart but the Conservancy's 2011 follow-up confirmed visitor patterns remained stable. For live foot traffic, NYC DOT conducts bi-annual pedestrian counts at 114 city locations, though none are inside the park perimeter.
