Chapter 1 · Human Geography

A park divided by footfall.

Central Park hosts 22 million visitors a year — but they don't distribute evenly. The CPC Visitor Survey reveals stark intensity gradients driven by transit access, landmarks, and hotel proximity.

22M
Annual visitors to Central Park
The Southern Gravity

You arrive from the south.

The Wollman Rink, Southwest Corner, and the Zoo receive over 500,000 visits per acre per year — the highest density in the park. Three subway lines and the Plaza Hotel funnel an extraordinary concentration of foot traffic through the southeast corner.

58%
Of visits enter south of 72nd Street
The Gradient

Foot traffic fades northward.

Moving north of 72nd Street, intensity drops dramatically. The Great Lawn and Reservoir receive moderate traffic. Above 86th Street, the North Woods and Great Hill see a fraction of the southern density.

"The southern third of the park accounts for a majority of all visits, while northern zones remain relatively quiet." — CPC Visitor Survey, 2009
The Quiet North

Above 86th — a different world.

Harlem Meer, the Great Hill, and the North Woods exist in a different register entirely. Low intensity means different behavior from every living thing in the park — including the squirrels who live there year-round.

4×
Fewer visitors per acre above 86th Street vs southern zones

Chapter 2 · Squirrel Geography

3,023 squirrels, one October.

Volunteer spotters covered every hectare of the park over 10 days. Unlike humans, squirrel sightings distribute relatively evenly — but their behavior varies sharply across the same gradient.

3,023
Sightings across 10 October survey days
The Approach Zone

Southern squirrels approach humans.

In the high-traffic southern zones, squirrels have been desensitized — and often actively trained by handfeeding. The approach rate in the south is 4× higher than in the quiet north.

4×
More likely to approach in high-traffic southern zones
The Flight Zone

Northern squirrels flee the scene.

In low-traffic northern zones — North Woods, Harlem Meer — squirrels treat humans as genuine threats. The flee rate in the north is significantly higher than the park-wide average.

"The squirrel is the same animal. But the behavioral profile is shaped entirely by how many humans it has encountered." — NYC Squirrel Census field notes
